
One thing we don’t usually think about at first is how design directly impacts our lives. Everything around us was created by someone with a specific design so it can perform an action.

A keyboard, for example, has buttons for our fingers and symbols to identify letters. This helps us connect words and create sentences. At first, these purposes are good: something is designed to fulfill a specific goal.

But...

What happens when the purpose is not the right one?**

The truth is that people who design technology have a big responsibility. Their designs can help people grow, but they can also push them to develop unhealthy habits in front of a screen. This leads to a natural question:

Where does good design end and manipulation begin?**

There are many real examples of manipulative design:

The main one that set a trend is TikTok. Its design is focused only on consuming more and more fast content. It is a design that traps the user's brain with so much dopamine that it creates a cycle for millions of people (including me).

Other cases are social media platforms. They are meant for human relationships, but the use of "reactions" can make life very difficult for teenagers. In more serious cases, they can even promote extremist ideas.

As a big fan of video games, I have several complaints about systems that work against the user. Games with "in-app purchases" often encourage, or even force, the user to spend money. As consumers, we are affected by these patterns, and it feels like we lose our value as humans and become just "buying machines."

As a developer, there is a lot of pressure on our shoulders. In the end, you want your product to work. The question is: how far will I go to achieve that?
